LEARNING OUTCOME 1 — Understand the structure and function of the circulatory system
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The learner can:
1.1 Describe the function of the heart
1.2 Describe how blood moves through the four chambers of the heart
1.3 Describe systemic and pulmonary circulation
1.4 Describe the structure and functions of blood vessels
1.5 Explain what blood pressure is
1.6 Identify blood pressure classifications

Assessment guidance:
1.1 Learners must include that the function of the heart is to pump blood throughout the body, supplying oxygen and nutrients to the tissues and removing carbon dioxide and other waste products.
1.2 Learners could produce a flowchart which identifies the four chambers of the heart and the route taken by the blood through them.
1.3 Learners could produce a diagram to describe the stages of systemic and pulmonary circulation. This must include the key blood vessels, the valves of the heart and the direction of blood flow.
1.4 Learners must describe the structure and functions of the major vessels of the body, including veins, venules, arteries, arterioles and capillaries.
1.5 Learners must explain what blood pressure is and what systolic and diastolic measures are.
1.6 Learners must identify blood pressure classifications using a reliable source such as the British Hypertension Society classification of blood pressure levels.
Any diagrams used must be labelled by the learner.

LEARNING OUTCOME 3 — Understand the structure and function of the skeleton
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The learner can:
3.1 Describe the basic functions of the skeleton
3.2 Identify the bones of the axial and appendicular skeleton
3.3 Identify the classification of bones
3.4 Describe the main features of a long bone
3.5 Describe the stages of bone growth
3.6 Describe posture in terms of:
    - curves of the spine
    - neutral spine alignment
    - movement potential of the spine
    - postural deviations

Assessment guidance:
3.1 Learners must describe the functions of the skeleton to include: protection, shape, support, blood cell production, attachment site for muscles (movement), storage and release of minerals.
3.2 Axial skeleton: cranium, cervical vertebrae, thoracic vertebrae, lumbar vertebrae, sacral vertebrae, sternum, ribs and coccyx. Appendicular skeleton: scapula, clavicle, humerus, ulna, radius, carpals, metacarpals, phalanges, pelvic girdle (pelvis), femur, patella, tibia, fibula, tarsals and metatarsals.
3.3 Classifications: long, short, flat, irregular and sesamoid.
3.4 Features of a long bone: articular cartilage, epiphysis, diaphysis, periosteum, epiphyseal growth plates, medullary cavity and bone marrow.
3.5 Must include the roles of the epiphyseal growth plates and the cells involved — osteoblasts, osteoclasts and osteocytes.
3.6 Posture:
  - Curves of the spine: healthy spine has 4 curves. Lumbar and cervical curves anteriorly convex; thoracic and sacral curves anteriorly concaved.
  - Neutral spine: position in which all of the curves are balanced and weight is evenly distributed.
  - Potential movement of the spine: flexion, extension, rotation, lateral flexion and reduction.
  - Postural deviations: kyphosis, lordosis, scoliosis.

LEARNING OUTCOME 4 — Understand joints in the skeleton
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The learner can:
4.1 Identify the classifications of joints
4.2 Describe the structure of synovial joints
4.3 Identify the types of synovial joints and their range of motion
4.4 Describe joint movement potential and joint actions
4.5 Identify the anatomical planes of movement
4.6 Define the anatomical terms of location
4.7 Describe the exercise variables that can have an effect on biomechanics and kinesiology

Assessment guidance:
4.1 Classifications: immovable/fixed, slightly moveable/cartilaginous and freely movable/synovial.
4.2 Structure of synovial joints: articulating bones, joint capsule, synovial membrane, synovial fluid, ligaments, tendons and cartilage.
4.3 Types: hinge, pivot, ball and socket, saddle, condyloid and gliding, with their associated movements.
4.4 Joint movement: flexion/extension, adduction/abduction, circumduction, supination/pronation, plantar flexion/dorsiflexion, lateral flexion and reduction, horizontal flexion/extension, elevation/depression. Major joints: ankle, knee, hip, shoulder, elbow, wrist.
4.5 Planes of motion: sagittal, frontal (coronal), transverse.
4.6 Terms of location: distal, proximal, anterior, posterior, inferior, superior, deep, superficial, medial, lateral.
4.7 Exercise variables: levers, centre of gravity, momentum, force.

8.3 Explain the 'all or none law' / motor unit recruitment
